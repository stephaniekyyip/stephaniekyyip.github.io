Electronic Rain Gauge

Senior design project sponsored by Glacier Peak Institute



Overview

As a part of my year-long senior project, I worked in an interdisciplinary team comprised of environmental science and electrical/ computer engineering students. Our sponsor, Glacier Peak Institute, tasked us with creating an environmental sensor curriculum for middle school students in Darrington, WA. The requirements were that the curriculum had to be low-cost, fun and interesting for the kids, and involve the outdoors. We decided to create an electronic rain gauge curriculum due to the high levels of rainfall in Darrington and our faculty advisor's prior experience in constructing a rain gauge.

How the Rain Gauge Works

The physical design of the rain gauge involved a funnel attached to a PVC pipe that allowed water to be collected in the PVC. At the bottom of the PVC pipe was a pressure sensor used to find the pressure of the rainwater, which was encased in a waterproofed container. Wires from the pressure sensor were threaded through a waterproof box to connect to the breadboard inside that contains the rest of the electronic components. The second pressure sensor on the breadboard is used to find the atmospheric pressure.

The two pressure sensors are used to find a differential pressure, which can be used to to find the depth of rainwater collected in a PVC tube after calibration. This pressure measurement, along with the current time measured by the Real Time Clock (RTC), was logged onto a micro SD card at a set interval of 15 minutes. The idea is that the rain gauge can be set outside to automatically collect rain data with minimal maintenance, which includes retrieving the SD card to collect the data and changing the batteries.

My Role within the Project

I interfaced various electronic components with the microcontroller using SPI and I2C and wrote Arduino code to control the components. I worked on the construction of the rain gauge and designed the waterproof housing for the pressure sensors. Additionally, I played a leadership role in the project by organizing meetings and communicating with the facuty advisors and project sponsor on behalf of the team.

Smart Light System

Final project in my Internet of Things class



Overview

For the final project in our Internet of Things course, my team decided to create a smart light system to remotely control a LED bulb. The idea behind the smart light was to conserve energy by being able to remotely turn off lights and adjust the brightness depending on the ambient light.

How the Smart Light Works

We interfaced the LED bulb, along with a photoresistor and a transistor, with a Raspberry Pi. The photoresistor was used to determine the ambient brightness of the room in order to adjust the LED brightness accordingly. The transistor used with the battery pack allowed us to vary the brightness of the LED using the pulse width modulation (PWM) output from the Raspberry Pi.

My Role within the Project

I was responsible for the software side of the project. In order to control the LED, I wrote Python scripts to adjust the LED brightness by varying the PWM output. I also wrote a Python script to read the input from the photoresistor to allow the option of automatically adjusting the brightness depending on the ambient light in the room. After setting up an Apache server on the Raspberry Pi, I created the user interface using HTML for users to select different LED settings and used PHP to retrieve the user input in order to execute the Python scripts.

Automated Dancing Puppet

Third year electrical engineering lab project


Overview

As a part of my electrical engineering labs during junior year, I worked with a lab partner to implement a marionette puppet that performed a dance routine on command. The idea behind the project was to become familiar with lab equipment and to develop engineering troubleshooting and teamwork skills by working towards a project throughout the school year.

How the Puppet Works

The puppet’s movements were controlled by various circuits and programmed in C using a Raspberry Pi. A motor and a connected optical encoder allowed for the control of the puppet’s limbs, which included moving each limb up or down by a designated distance.

My Role in the Project

Though the lab instructors had done the final design for both the hardware and software, my lab partner and I were responsible for the construction, testing, and troubleshooting of the puppet. We had to read circuit schematics, build and test the circuits and hardware, modify C code to control the puppet’s dance moves, and integrate and troubleshoot both the software and hardware. I focused mainly on the software side of the project amd worked together with my partner on other areas of the project, particularly the troubleshooting and testing aspects.
